Readiness score — how it's calculated

Evidence-based formula validated for trained endurance athletes. Four inputs, weighted by predictive accuracy:

Form (TSB) — fitness minus fatigue45%
HRV 7-day trend — vs your normal range25%
A:C ratio — load balance & injury risk20%
Deep sleep — vs 90 min target10%

TSB dominates because it's the strongest performance predictor for trained endurance athletes (Banister impulse-response model). HRV trend captures daily recovery sensitivity. A:C ratio guards against injury risk. Sleep has the lowest weight — consumer devices measure it imprecisely and one bad night matters less than cumulative patterns.

Fitness (CTL - Chronic Training Load)

7-week rolling average

CTL measures your fitness - the average training load over the past 42 days. Higher = more fit. It rises slowly with consistent training and falls during rest or taper. Think of it as your aerobic engine size.

Sync to load.

Fatigue (ATL - Acute Training Load)

7-day rolling average

ATL measures your fatigue - the average training load over the past 7 days. Higher = more fatigued. It responds quickly to hard training and drops fast with rest. A high ATL relative to CTL means you're carrying fatigue.

Sync to load.

Form (TSB - Training Stress Balance)

Fitness minus Fatigue

TSB = CTL - ATL. It tells you how fresh you are. Negative = carrying fatigue. Positive = fresh and rested. The sweet spot for racing is +5 to +15 - fit but not rusty. Below -25 = high injury risk. Above +20 = detrained.

Sync to load.

Weekly Training Load (Intervals.icu)

True training load

Intervals.icu's Training Load combines duration and intensity relative to your set thresholds (pace/HR) into a single TSS-style number per session. A 5-mile easy run and a 3-mile race can look similar by distance, but Load correctly shows the race cost far more. It's a more accurate measure of training stress than miles alone.

Sync to see Load breakdown.

A:C Ratio (Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio)

Injury risk indicator

A:C = ATL / CTL. Compares your recent load to your long-term load. Optimum range: 0.8-1.3. Below 0.8 = undertraining. Above 1.3 = elevated injury risk. Above 1.5 = high injury risk.

VO₂max

Intervals.icu + Garmin

Maximum oxygen consumption — the single best indicator of aerobic fitness. Intervals.icu doesn't run its own VO2max model; when a value is present it's usually pulled from a synced device estimate. Garmin estimates from pace-to-HR ratio via FirstBeat algorithm. If Intervals.icu doesn't have a value for a given sync, only Garmin's is shown. Track over time rather than comparing sources directly.

Aerobic decoupling

Per run

How much HR drifts relative to pace over a run. Under 5% = strong aerobic base. Under 2% = excellent. The most direct predictor of ultra endurance performance — low decoupling means you hold pace efficiently over long distances.
